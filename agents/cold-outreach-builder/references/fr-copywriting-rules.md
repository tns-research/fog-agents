# French copywriting rules

Ten mandatory rules for French cold outreach copy. These are non-negotiable. Apply during drafting (`copywriting-fr` skill) and during review (`reviewing-cold-email` skill).

These rules are linguistic, not stylistic preferences. A naive English-to-French translation will violate every one of them and will sound like spam to a French B2B reader.

---

## 1. Kill the text that adds nothing

If a sentence is removed and the meaning of the message holds, the sentence was filler. Cold email has 80 words to land. Every sentence earns its place.

**Banned**: introductory throat-clearing ("Avant tout, je tenais à vous remercier de prendre le temps de lire ce message"), apologies for length, generic compliments ("Votre profil est très inspirant"), recap of the recipient's role they already know.

**Apply**: read the draft. For each sentence, ask "if I delete this, does the message still work?" If yes, delete.

---

## 2. Nobody cares about you

The reader cares about their pain, their goal, their context. They do not care about your company, your story, your product roadmap. Open with their world, not yours.

**Banned**: "Chez [Company], nous sommes spécialisés dans...", "Notre solution permet de...", "Avec plus de X clients, nous...".

**Apply**: first sentence references the prospect (a post they wrote, a hire they made, a metric they shared, a problem in their vertical). Their name and company appear before yours.

---

## 3. No BS words

The reader is fluent in corporate French and has built immunity to filler vocabulary. Every BS word is a trust drop.

**Banned (corporate calques translated from English)**: synergiser, solutionner, impacter, leverager, optimiser (overused), rationaliser, opérationnaliser, démultiplier, pivoter (out of startup context).

**Banned (vague qualifiers)**: innovant, disruptif, révolutionnaire, unique en son genre, leader sur son marché, à la pointe, stratégique (when meaningless).

**Apply**: replace with concrete verbs. "Nous synergisons les équipes commerciales" becomes "On a aidé deux équipes commerciales à arrêter de se marcher dessus sur les leads."

---

## 4. Kill adverbs

Adverbs are the writer's hedge. The right verb does not need an adverb.

**Banned**: vraiment, réellement, particulièrement, absolument, totalement, complètement (when used to amplify weak verbs).

**Apply**: "Nous sommes vraiment intéressés" becomes "On veut en parler". "Cela fonctionne particulièrement bien pour..." becomes "Cela résout exactement [pain spécifique] pour..."

---

## 5. Active voice, not passive

Passive voice in French is heavier than in English and signals corporate distance. The doer of the action should be at the front of the sentence.

**Banned**: "Une démonstration vous sera proposée", "Votre profil a été identifié par notre équipe", "Un suivi sera assuré".

**Apply**: "Je vous propose une démo de 15 min", "J'ai vu votre post sur X", "Je reviens vers vous lundi".

---

## 6. No fake closeness

Pretending to know the reader is worse than admitting you do not. Fake warmth is the most reliable spam signal in French B2B.

**Banned**: "J'espère que ce message vous trouve bien" (direct translation of an English AI-tell, sounds wrong in French), "Je suis tombé sur votre profil" (overused), "Cher [first name]" when you would normally say "Bonjour [first name]" or vouvoyer the reader, fake personal references ("Comme nous partageons une passion commune pour...").

**Apply**: state your reason for writing in the first sentence. "Je vois que vous avez levé chez X la semaine dernière. Vous embauchez sur Y, voici ce qu'on fait pour les boîtes à votre stade."

---

## 7. No "Pas pour [X]" framing

Negative framing ("pas pour les startups", "pas pour les freelances") is weak and reads as defensive. Always rewrite as a direct positive assertion of who the offer is for.

**Banned**: "Pas pour les freelances", "Pas pour les boîtes en seed", "Ce n'est pas un outil pour...".

**Apply**: "Pour les SaaS B2B entre 1M et 10M ARR" instead of "Pas pour les early-stage". State the audience, not the exclusions.

---

## 8. Concrete numbers, not vague claims

Numbers anchor. Vague claims slide off. If you cannot put a number on a claim, the claim is not strong enough to ship.

**Banned**: "beaucoup de clients", "une amélioration significative", "un retour sur investissement rapide", "des résultats notables".

**Apply**: "37 clients SaaS B2B en 2025", "passé de 12% à 28% de réponse", "ROI sur 4 mois en moyenne", "réduit de 6h à 45min par semaine".

---

## 9. Length targets per message type

French sentences are longer than English on average. Stick to strict word caps to compensate.

| Message type | Word target | Hard ceiling |
|--------------|-------------|--------------|
| Cold email body | 60 to 90 | 110 |
| Subject line | 4 to 7 words | 10 (Gmail FR truncates around 33-50 chars) |
| LinkedIn connection note | 30 to 40 words | 50 (LinkedIn cap is 200 chars) |
| LinkedIn DM (first) | 40 to 60 | 80 |
| LinkedIn voice note transcript equivalent | 90 to 120 | 150 |

**Apply**: count words. If over the ceiling, kill text per rule 1.

---

## 10. Single CTA, action-shaped

The reader's brain stops at the first ambiguity. One ask per message. The ask should be a sentence the reader can answer with yes or no, not a process.

**Banned (multiple CTAs)**: "Vous pouvez répondre, ou bien réserver via le lien, ou nous écrire à hello@...".

**Banned (vague)**: "Tenez-moi au courant", "N'hésitez pas si cela vous parle", "Faisons connaissance".

**Apply (binary asks)**: "Ça vous intéresse, oui ou non ?", "On se cale 15 min jeudi à 14h ?", "Je vous envoie le doc, ok ?".
