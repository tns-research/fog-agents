# Ad Library: Creative Briefs Reference

Reference library of advertising styles, formats, and examples. Use for inspiration when generating the 15 creative prompts. The agent may also invent entirely new ideas; this library is not exhaustive.

---

## 0. Creative as targeting: the 2025 context

In 2025-2026, Meta's algorithm uses the creative itself as the primary targeting signal. The ad reaches the audience that resonates with it, not the audience the media buyer defines. This means:

- **Creative IS the targeting.** A diverse creative pool (different angles, formats, personas) reaches different audience segments automatically. Running 15 briefs with identical tone and visual direction is equivalent to targeting only one audience.
- **Ad families, not ad variants.** Instead of slightly tweaking the same ad, produce genuinely different creative executions of the same core product. A coffee brand should have: a meme-style problem hook, a testimonial screenshot, an ingredient origin visual, a typography-only card, and a product hero, each will find a different segment.
- **The algorithm rewards engagement signals.** Scroll-stop rate, click-through, and 3-second hold time determine distribution. The creative is the variable. Generate concepts that earn these signals, not concepts that look polished but read as ads.

## 1. Proven concept angles: by funnel stage

Named, actionable angles. Each has a one-line description, the psychological mechanism, and a copy example. Use these as starting points, not scripts.

**Important, French examples below:** The FR examples are *illustrations of the angle*, not copy templates. They were written for a coffee brand and may use fragment structures that worked in that context. When writing copy for a different brand, **think the idea natively in French as a complete sentence with a verb and a point of view** (see Section 4.1). Do not copy the syntactic structure of these examples, copy the *strategic idea* and express it in natural, idiomatic French for the target brand.

### TOFU: awareness angles

#### 1.1 The Anti-Category
Challenge what every brand in the category does. Don't name a competitor, name the category convention.
- **Mechanism:** Pattern interruption; the reader recognises the category lie and feels relief at the truth.
- **Example (EN):** "Every coffee brand talks about strength. We only care about taste."
- **Example (FR):** "Toutes les marques de café parlent de force. Chez nous, c'est le goût qui compte."

#### 1.2 The Specific Ritual
Don't say "morning ritual." Name the exact sensory moment that insiders recognise: the second crack of the grinder, the smell before the brew, the quiet at 6am when no one else is awake.
- **Mechanism:** Recognition triggers belonging, this ad is for *me*.
- **Example (FR):** "Ce moment où ça commence à sentir bon, avant même d'y toucher."

#### 1.3 The Wrong Answer
State the common belief, then subvert it in the subheadline. The contrast is the idea.
- **Mechanism:** Cognitive dissonance → reappraisal → brand association with the truth.
- **Example (FR):** "Un café fort, c'est un bon café." / "Pas si on a raté la torréfaction."

#### 1.4 The Origin Number
One concrete, specific number that makes the origin feel real, not aspirational. Specificity = credibility.
- **Mechanism:** Numbers anchor abstractions. 1800m feels different from "high altitude." 48 hours feels different from "fresh."
- **Example (FR):** "1 800 mètres d'altitude. C'est là que commence le goût."

#### 1.5 The Quiet Flex
No superlatives. No exclamation marks. Just specificity so precise it signals quality without stating it.
- **Mechanism:** Restraint signals confidence. Luxury brands don't shout.
- **Example (FR):** "On expédie 48 h après torréfaction. Pas 48 jours."

#### 1.6 The Parallel
Draw an analogy to something the audience already values and understand as non-negotiable.
- **Mechanism:** Transfers existing belief (bread = fresh daily) to new category (coffee = fresh always).
- **Example (FR):** "Votre boulanger fait votre pain le matin. Votre café devrait être pareil."

#### 1.7 The Insider
Speak to the audience as if they already know the category deeply. Exclude the mass market; invite the discerning.
- **Mechanism:** Flattery through assumption; makes the reader feel seen.
- **Example (FR):** "Vous savez la différence entre 30 et 90 secondes d'extraction. Nous aussi."

#### 1.8 The Quiet Confession
Admit a deliberate limitation as a sign of quality, not a weakness. Anti-marketing voice.
- **Mechanism:** Counterintuitive honesty disarms skepticism; signals authenticity.
- **Example (FR):** "On ne torréfie pas beaucoup. On torréfie bien."

### MOFU: consideration angles

#### 1.9 The Process Reveal
Explain one specific step in your process that the industry skips. Educate without lecturing.
- **Mechanism:** Education creates investment; understanding the process creates preference.
- **Example (FR):** "La plupart des cafés sont torréfiés des mois avant d'arriver chez vous. Le nôtre : 48 heures."

#### 1.10 The False Choice Collapse
There's a trade-off people assume exists (quality vs. price; organic vs. taste; local vs. convenience). Show it doesn't.
- **Mechanism:** Relieving cognitive dissonance, "I don't have to choose."
- **Example (FR):** "Équitable et vraiment bon. Pas l'un ou l'autre."

#### 1.11 The Skeptic Disarm
Anticipate the "too good to be true" reaction. Address the objection head-on in the copy.
- **Mechanism:** Naming the objection defuses it. Feels honest, not defensive.
- **Example (FR):** "Oui, on torréfie vraiment à la commande. Non, ce n'est pas une formule marketing."

#### 1.12 The Selection Proof
Show the ratio of inputs to outputs as a signal of curation. 200 lots tasted, 12 kept.
- **Mechanism:** Effort and selectivity signal quality without claiming "the best."
- **Example (FR):** "200 lots goûtés cette saison. On en a gardé 12."

#### 1.13 The Expert Voice (without the celebrity)
Not a famous name, an unnamed craftsperson with a specific, eccentric qualification.
- **Mechanism:** Specificity of expertise is more credible than name-dropping.
- **Example (EN):** "Our head roaster trained in Yemen, Colombia, and Ethiopia. She rejects 94% of what she tastes. That's the point."

#### 1.14 The Comparison Without a Competitor
Contrast against a category behaviour without naming anyone.
- **Mechanism:** The contrast makes the brand's position clear without attacking; the reader fills in the competitor.
- **Example (FR):** "Pendant que d'autres cherchent à vendre plus, on cherche à torréfier mieux."

### BOFU: conversion angles

#### 1.15 The Easy First Step
Frame the subscribe/buy as smaller than it feels. Then hint that most people don't want to leave.
- **Mechanism:** Lowers perceived commitment; reverse psychology on churn.
- **Example (FR):** "Essayez une fois. Annulez quand vous voulez. La plupart oublient, parce qu'ils ne veulent plus partir."

#### 1.16 The Social Proof with Specificity
Not a percentage. A specific, concrete number that represents real people doing a specific thing.
- **Mechanism:** Specificity makes social proof feel real, not manufactured.
- **Example (FR):** "4 200 abonnés. 18 mois de livraisons sans une commande ratée."

#### 1.17 The Cost Reframe
Show the price relative to what the audience already spends without hesitation (coffee shop visit, streaming, etc.).
- **Mechanism:** Relative pricing reduces sticker shock; anchors the value.
- **Example (FR):** "Moins cher que deux expressos en terrasse par semaine. À déguster chez vous."

#### 1.18 The Guarantee as Headline
Lead with the safety net, not the product. Make the risk of trying zero.
- **Mechanism:** Removes the last objection for someone already interested.
- **Example (FR):** "Si ce n'est pas à votre goût, on remplace. Sans formulaire."

#### 1.19 The Absence Cost
What does NOT acting cost? What happens on the Monday when they run out?
- **Mechanism:** Loss aversion is stronger than gain motivation at conversion stage.
- **Example (FR):** "Sans date de livraison, vous tomberez en panne un lundi matin."

#### 1.20 The Micro-Commitment
Ask for something so small it's hard to say no. The first yes leads to the next.
- **Mechanism:** Foot-in-the-door; behavioural commitment consistency.
- **Example (FR):** "Un abonnement. Un sac. Un essai. Commencez là."

## 2. Headline and copy structures

### 2.1 Problem-Agitate-Solve (PAS)
- **Pattern:** State problem → amplify pain → introduce solution.
- **Example (EN):** "Tired of X? Every day you wait, Y gets worse. Here's what actually works."
- **Example (FR):** "Vous en avez marre de X ? Chaque jour, Y s'aggrave. Voici ce qui marche vraiment."

### 2.2 Before-After
- **Pattern:** Before (problem state) vs after (with product).
- **Example:** "Before: [frustration]. After: [outcome]."

### 2.3 Question hook
- **Pattern:** Open with a question the target asks themselves.
- **Example (EN):** "Why do [target] still struggle with [problem]?"
- **Example (FR):** "Pourquoi [cible] galèrent encore avec [problème] ?"

### 2.4 Number / list
- **Pattern:** "X reasons / ways / signs / mistakes…", only if each item has weight; don't pad.
- **Example:** "3 mistakes that keep [problem] from going away."

### 2.5 Contrarian / anti-advice
- **Pattern:** Challenge common belief; position product as the exception.
- **Example:** "Forget what you've heard. [New rule]. Here's why."

### 2.6 Story open
- **Pattern:** Short anecdote or scenario, then lesson or product.
- **Example:** "Last year I [situation]. Then I discovered [product]. Now [result]."

### 2.7 Direct promise
- **Pattern:** One clear outcome in one sentence.
- **Example (EN):** "Finally, [benefit] without [old drawback]."
- **Example (FR):** "Enfin [bénéfice] sans [ancien inconvénient]."

### 2.8 Urgency / scarcity (use sparingly)
- **Pattern:** Limited time, stock, or access, only when true.
- **Example:** "Only X left." / "Until [date]."

## 3. Visual and format vocabulary

### 3.1 Static ad formats
- **Vertical 4:5**: Feed-friendly (Meta, social).
- **Square 1:1**: Universal.
- **Carousel frame**: One clear idea per frame; consistent style.
- **Story 9:16**: Full screen, bold type, short copy.
- **Typography-as-hero**: Plain or gradient background; type is the primary visual. No product. Used when the idea is the message (Anti-Category angle, Quiet Flex).

### 3.2 Camera position: be specific
State both height and angle. Do not write "standard shot."

| Height | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Low angle (below subject) | Drama, scale, dominance. Used for products to make them feel larger, weightier. |
| Eye level | Equality, naturalism, documentary feel. |
| Slightly elevated (30°-45° above) | Flattery, clarity; common for food/coffee overhead or near-overhead. |
| True overhead (flat lay) | Abstract, editorial, product-centric. Strong graphic quality. |
| Dutch tilt (camera rotated) | Instability, energy, dynamism. Use rarely; only when tension is the point. |

Angle from subject:
- **Frontal:** Direct, confrontational, flat, works for typography-hero ads.
- **3/4 front:** Most common; shows form and depth.
- **Profile:** Elegance, mystery, movement.
- **45° above and front:** Common product hero; gives three-dimensionality.

### 3.3 Focal length equivalents: include one
| Focal length | Visual character |
|--------------|-----------------|
| 16-24mm wide | Environmental, expansive, slight distortion at edges. Pulls in context. |
| 35mm | Natural, cinematic. Feels like the eye without drama. |
| 50mm standard | Clean, neutral. The product is neither flattered nor challenged. |
| 85mm portrait | Gentle compression; flattering. Subject separated from background. The go-to for premium product hero. |
| 100mm+ macro | Extreme surface detail; texture, grain, liquid. |
| 200mm telephoto | Heavy compression; subject isolated; background rendered as soft color. |

### 3.4 Lighting: specify source, direction, and quality
Never write only "warm light" or "soft light." State three elements:

**Source:** window / overcast sky / studio softbox / bare strobe / practical lamp / candle / backlight
**Direction:** camera-left 45° / overhead / backlit / rim / split (half lit, half shadow)
**Quality:** soft diffuse (wide source close to subject) / medium / hard specular (small or distant source)

| Lighting type | Character | Good for |
|--------------|-----------|----------|
| Soft window light, camera-left 45° | Artisan, honest, quiet luxury | TOFU brand, ritual, origin |
| Overhead soft (flat lay) | Editorial, clean, graphic | MOFU product comparison, BOFU hero |
| Backlit (rim/halo) | Cinematic, premium, warmth | TOFU aspirational lifestyle |
| Hard side light (split) | Drama, craft, character | Ingredient close-up, roastery shots |
| Practical lamp (ambient) | Intimacy, home, ritual | Morning scenes, lifestyle |
| Cool diffuse (overcast/studio) | Clinical, precise, modern | BOFU pack shot, technical claim |

### 3.5 Color grade: state the treatment
| Treatment | Character |
|-----------|-----------|
| Warm + matte (lifted blacks) | Artisan, vintage, analog feel |
| Neutral + clean digital | Premium, modern DTC |
| High contrast + rich shadows | Drama, luxury, coffee noir |
| Cool + desaturated | Clarity, credibility, clinical |
| Film grain + fade | Authentic, UGC-adjacent, honest |
| Minimal monochrome | Typography-hero; the copy is the creative |

### 3.6 What to exclude: state it explicitly
Always add one "exclude" clause to the prompt. It removes the most predictable cliché.
- "No coffee cup in frame."
- "No steam or smoke."
- "No marble surface."
- "No coffee beans scattered decoratively."
- "No human hands in frame."
- "No latte art."

### 3.8 Aperture language: depth of field as a design tool
Include an aperture reference to signal the depth-of-field character. This affects whether background is soft (product isolated) or sharp (environmental context).

|----------|---------------|---------|
| f/1.4-f/1.8 | Extremely shallow, subject sharp, background dissolved | Faces, single-hero products, isolation |
| f/2.8 | Shallow, subject and immediate context sharp | Portrait with lifestyle context |
| f/5.6 | Medium, subject and near-background visible | Product in context, some environment |
| f/8 | Deep, most of scene in focus | Product hero with environment, origin scenes |
| f/11 | Maximum sharpness throughout | Commercial product photography, pack shots |
| Macro with f/2.8 | Extreme close-up, thin focus plane | Surface texture, ingredient detail |

### 3.9 Color consistency technique for batch production
When generating a batch of 15 briefs, maintaining brand color across all images requires explicit prompting. Use these techniques:

1. **Color temperature as proxy**: More reliable than hex codes for most generators:
   - "Warm tungsten light, 3200K" → pushes amber and gold tones
   - "Overcast daylight, 6500K, neutral cool" → muted Scandinavian palette
   - "Golden hour, warm 4000K" → lifestyle warmth
   - "Daylight-balanced studio strobes, 5500K" → clean commercial white
2. **Color palette template**: Add a palette block to every prompt in the batch: `"COLOR PALETTE: warm cream, deep forest green, no cool blues, no stark white, no black backgrounds"`
3. **Film stock as color anchor**: All 15 briefs specifying "Kodak Portra 400 film simulation" will share warm, skin-forward color character across the batch
4. **Negative color prompts**: Explicitly exclude off-brand colors: `"--no neon, no cool gray, no purple"` (Midjourney) or `"avoid any cool blue tones"` (GPT-4o/DALL-E)

## 4. French copy: idioms and tone

Use when `copy_language` is `fr`. Prefer natural French; avoid literal translation of English slogans.

**Rule: one clear idea per line.** Headline and subheadline must each be a complete, clear idea. No telegraphic fragments that don't mean anything. Prefer a full sentence or a slogan that a native speaker understands in one read.

### 4.1 The cardinal rule: think in French, write a sentence

**A French headline is a sentence with a point of view, not a list of words separated by periods.**

English copywriting rewards telegraphic fragments ("Fresh. Local. Yours."). French does not. In French, fragments without a verb read as a shopping list, not as an idea. The rhythm of French copy comes from syntax, the placement of the verb, the balance of the clause, not from punctuation tricks.

**Mandatory process when `copy_language` is `fr`:**
1. Formulate the idea as a complete French sentence first. Subject + verb + object/complement.
2. Read it aloud. Does it have the cadence of something a French creative director would present? If it sounds like it was translated from a two-word English tagline, start over.
3. Only then, shorten if possible, but never at the cost of losing the verb or the idea. A 12-word sentence that means something beats a 4-word fragment that means nothing.

**Structures that work in French (all contain a verb or a complete thought):**

- **The confession with a verb:** "On ne torréfie pas beaucoup, mais on torréfie bien.", honest, anti-marketing. The verb "torréfier" repeated creates rhythm. The "mais" carries the idea.
- **The insider question:** "Vous sentez la différence entre 30 et 90 secondes d'extraction ?", a real question with a subject and a verb. Flattering, specific.
- **The deliberate imperative:** "Buvez mieux, c'est à votre portée.", direct, but the imperative IS a verb. Complete thought.
- **The contrast with a point of view:** "On pourrait vous vendre du café industriel. On a choisi de torréfier le nôtre.", two clauses, a choice, a position.
- **The analogy:** "Votre boulanger fait votre pain le matin. Votre café devrait être aussi frais.", full sentence, the analogy creates the idea.
- **The quiet promise:** "Votre meilleur café vous attend, à partir de 20 € par mois.", one sentence, specific, no fluff.
- **The surprising observation:** "Le café que vous buvez a été torréfié il y a trois mois. Le nôtre a été torréfié ce matin.", two complete sentences, the contrast IS the idea.

**Structures that DO NOT work (and that the agent must never produce):**

- ❌ "Du grain, du temps, du goût.", Three nouns without a verb. A spec sheet, not an idea. What about the grain? What about the time? No point of view.
- ❌ "Torréfié ce matin. Chez vous demain.", Two telegram fragments. Sounds like a delivery tracking notification, not a headline.
- ❌ "Un café. Un abonnement. Un essai.", Dot-separated noun list. English tagline structure forced into French.
- ❌ "150 g. Toute la famille. Tout l'été.", Numbers + fragments. Packaging copy, not advertising.
- ❌ "Bio. Artisanal. Français.", Adjective list. Could describe any brand. Zero idea.
- ❌ "Votre meilleur café. À partir de 20 € par mois.", Two disconnected fragments pretending to be a thought. Combine them into a real sentence.

### 4.2 Headlines that "mean something"
- **Promise:** "Enfin un [produit] qui [bénéfice concret]."
- **Question:** "Et si [solution] ne tenait qu'à un détail ?"
- **Contrast:** "Pas besoin de [stéréotype]. Juste [vérité produit]."
- **Ritual:** "Le [moment] que vous attendiez."
- **Belonging:** "Pour ceux qui [valeur partagée]."

### 4.3 Tone by segment
- **Premium / luxury:** "L'art de…", "Un instant rare.", "Ce qui compte."
- **DTC / direct:** "Sans compromis.", "Vraiment pour vous.", "On a tout repensé."
- **Problem-aware:** "On sait ce que c'est.", "La fin de [problème].", "Enfin une réponse."

### 4.4 Avoid: the blacklist

These patterns are **forbidden**. If the agent produces any of them, the headline must be rewritten before finalising.

**Structural failures:**
- **Dot-separated fragment lists:** "Bio. Artisanal. Français." / "Un gel. Une plante. Une famille." / "150 ml. Toute la famille. Tout l'été.", These are English tagline structures. In French they read as a PowerPoint slide, not an idea. **Always rewrite as a sentence with a verb.**
- **Stat + colon as headline:** "100 % des testeurs : peau apaisée." / "94 % de jus frais.", A data point is not a headline. It is an ingredient for a headline. Wrap it in a sentence that has a point of view: "Cent pour cent des testeurs ont la peau apaisée, et personne n'y croyait au départ."
- **Promo banners disguised as headlines:** "Échantillon offert dès 25 €." / "Livraison gratuite dès 49 €.", These belong in a website banner, not in a creative. Build a headline around the benefit, not the mechanic.
- **Noun lists without a verb:** "Du grain, du temps, du goût.", What about them? No verb, no idea, no point of view. Rewrite: "Il faut du grain, du temps et du savoir-faire pour obtenir ce goût."
- **Fragments where the implied meaning is unclear:** "Bu demain." is incomplete. "Livré chez vous demain matin, torréfié cette nuit." is complete.

**Linguistic failures:**
- Calques from English ("Just do it" → "Fais-le" is weak; find a French idea. "Start here" → "Commencez par là" is a translation, not copy).
- Vague superlatives without substance ("Le meilleur", "Incroyable" alone).
- Overused marketing words without meaning ("innovation", "révolution" without proof).
- Generic category statements that any competitor could say: "Bio ne veut pas dire compromis.", Any bio brand can print this. Make it specific to the brand.

**Punctuation failures:**
- **Em-dashes (, ) in French copy:** Never use em-dashes in headlines, subheadlines, or primary text. Em-dashes are an English typographic convention. In French, use a spaced en-dash ( - ), a comma, a semicolon, or restructure the sentence. "Le gel natif, 94 % de pulpe" is wrong. "Le gel natif : 94 % de pulpe" or "Le gel natif, 94 % de pulpe fraîche" is correct.

**Accents and diacritics (mandatory when `copy_language` is `fr`):**
French copy MUST carry its full accents. They are valid, expected UTF-8 in the briefs JSON, the renderer prints `headline`/`subheadline` on the creative exactly as written, so a stripped accent ships as a typo. The complete set you will use:

- **Lowercase:** é è ê ë  ·  à â  ·  î ï  ·  ô ö  ·  ù û ü  ·  ç  ·  (rare: ÿ, œ, æ in "cœur", "œuf", "et cætera")
- **Uppercase:** É È Ê Ë  ·  À Â  ·  Î Ï  ·  Ô  ·  Ù Û  ·  Ç (accents on capitals are correct French, keep them: "Économisez", "À partir de", "Ça change tout")

Write "torréfié", "différence", "protège", "goûtés", "pilotés", "déjà", "français", "créé", "réglé", "qualité", never their ASCII-stripped forms. **This is the one place ASCII-only does not apply:** the only characters banned from French copy are the em/en dashes above. Everything diacritical stays.

**The diagnostic test:** Read the headline aloud in French. If it sounds like a voice-over for a translated American ad, it is wrong. If it sounds like something a friend would say to explain why they love this product, it is closer.

## 6. Real assets in the frame

Briefs attach the founder's real material via `reference_assets` (roles: `product`, `screenshot`, `logo`, `hero`). A physical-product brand leans on the packshot; a SaaS / app / service leans on the product UI `screenshot` and the brand `logo`. The framings below apply to whichever asset is the subject.

- **Hero shot:** Subject centre (packshot, or a UI screenshot inside a device mockup); clean background; label or screen content visible.
- **In context:** Asset in use or in a lifestyle shot; still recognisable.
- **Detail:** Close-up texture, ingredient, signature element, or a single UI feature callout.
- **Environmental:** Asset is secondary to the scene; proves it belongs in this lifestyle.
- **Logo lockup:** When there is no product or UI to show, the logo itself is the asset, a small corner mark or a centered lockup on a flat brand-colored field.
- **Rule for briefs:** If any real asset appears, the `image_prompt` and each `reference_assets[].instruction` must state: which attached image it is, fidelity ("use exactly as provided, do not redraw" for a `logo` or `product`; "do not invent or alter the interface" for a `screenshot`), placement, and a clean area for the text overlay if needed.

## 8. Headline quality tests

Every headline and subheadline must pass these five tests before finalising. If it fails any test, rewrite it.

### Test 1: The Competitor Test
Could a direct competitor say this exact line without changing a word? If yes, it is a category line, not a brand line. Make it specific to what only this brand can truthfully claim.

### Test 2: The 5-Word Test
Can you cut the headline to 5 words and keep the idea? If yes, cut it. Shorter is not always right, but it forces you to find the kernel. Then decide what to add back only if it adds meaning, not decoration.

### Test 3: The Surprising Word Test
What word in this headline is unexpected, not the word you'd predict given the first few words? If every word is predictable, the line is flat. Great headlines have at least one word that surprises. Find it; if it isn't there, rebuild around one.

### Test 4: The Emotion Test
What specific emotion does this line produce, relief, recognition, curiosity, desire, irony, pride? Name it. If the answer is "nothing specific," it's a fact sentence, not a headline. Facts inform; headlines move.

### Test 5: The Native Speaker Test
For French: read the line aloud. Does it have a natural cadence? Would a French creative director nod at it or wince? If it sounds like it was translated from English, it was. Start again in French.

## 9. Visual diversity mandate across 15 briefs

The 15 briefs must not all look like the same creative direction with different copy. The agent must enforce minimum variety across the batch. Use this as a checklist when planning.

### 9.1 Camera position: minimum 4 distinct setups across 15 briefs
No more than 3 briefs may share the same camera height + angle combination.

### 9.2 Lighting: minimum 4 distinct setups across 15 briefs
No more than 3 briefs may share the same lighting source + direction + quality combination.
- "Soft key light, camera-left" cannot appear more than 3 times.
- At least one brief should use hard or split lighting.
- At least one brief should be primarily lit by a practical or environmental source.

### 9.3 Background / environment: minimum 3 distinct types
Not all briefs can be on marble or wood. Require at least:
- 1 origin/environmental (farm, roastery, outdoor)
- 1 minimal/studio (neutral gradient, flat)
- 1 lifestyle/interior (home kitchen, café table, hands)
- Optionally: 1 typographic (flat background, type as the visual)

### 9.4 Subject distance: minimum 3 distinct distances
- **Wide/environmental:** Subject is a small element in a larger scene.
- **Medium/lifestyle:** Subject is the clear focus; environment provides context.
- **Close-up/detail:** Surface, texture, label, liquid, hands, no full-product packshot.

### 9.5 Cliché exclusion: forbid these in the batch
These are so common in coffee advertising they have become invisible. Each is a missed opportunity:
- Steam rising from a cup as the primary subject.
- Hands cupped around a mug with blurred background.
- Coffee beans scattered on a flat surface.
- Marble surface as the only background.
- Identical overhead flat-lay composition for every product hero.
If any of these appear, replace with a more specific, unexpected composition.

## 10. Mix of the 15 briefs

Aim for variety across the 15:
- **Angles:** Use different named angles from Section 1; do not use the same angle twice.
- **Formats:** Vary headline structure (question, promise, story, number, contrast, confession).
- **Product presence (e-commerce default):** The product must appear in at least 10-12 of the 15 creatives. Only 2-3 briefs may be product-free when the angle is purely brand/storytelling (e.g. origin story, journey).
- **Headline and subheadline:** Each brief has a headline (main line) and optionally a subheadline (secondary line). Use empty subheadline when one line is enough.
- **Tone:** Align with brand; vary intensity (quieter and more restrained vs. bolder and more direct) across briefs.
- **Visual:** Enforce Section 9 diversity mandate, camera, lighting, environment, distance all vary.

Use this library to inspire; do not limit to it. New concepts that fit the brand and research are encouraged.

## 11. What's dying: formats to avoid on Meta in 2025-2026

These are so overused they have become invisible. Each is a creative opportunity missed. If any of the 15 briefs defaults to one of these without a strong overriding reason, flag it and replace.

| Format / pattern | Why it's dying | What to do instead |
|-----------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Aspirational lifestyle stock photography | Generic couple on beach, woman laughing in kitchen, the brain filters these instantly; they signal "ad" | Real moment, specific sensory detail, or typographic hook |
| Designed "5-star review" graphic (polished brand version) | Format went mainstream 2022-23; now expected; trust signal is gone | Use the raw screenshot format (Section 1B.3), rawness is the mechanic |
| Pastel DTC aesthetic (Glossier-era) | Adopted uniformly by skincare, supplements, wellness, all ads look the same | High contrast, specific texture, or unexpected color |
| "Free shipping" as primary hook | Was effective 2018-2022; by 2025 free shipping is an expectation, not an offer | Lead with the product benefit or a specific proof point |
| Countdown timers and fake urgency | Audiences are sophisticated; manufactured urgency reduces trust, especially under 40 | Genuine scarcity only; or remove urgency and lead with value |
| Marble-and-wood product hero (only) | Beautiful, but used by every DTC coffee/wellness/skincare brand simultaneously | Different surface, different light, different environment, or no surface at all |
| Steam-rising-from-cup as primary subject | Coffee ad default since 2015. Invisible in 2025. | Show the bean, the roastery, the farm, the cold bag, the text, anything but steam |
| Founder story as cold-audience TOFU | Works briefly, then audiences saturate; doesn't scale to wide audiences | Reserve founder content for MOFU (when audience already knows the brand) |
| Comparison charts (brand vs. generic) | Over-deployed in supplements, skincare, SaaS; lost pattern-interrupt value | Process reveal, specific number, or the skeptic-disarm angle instead |
| "As seen in Forbes / NY Times" badge strip at TOFU | Still adds MOFU credibility; but as a TOFU scroll-stopper it's exhausted | Use it only at MOFU/BOFU as a trust layer, not as a TOFU hook |
| Multiple CTAs in one ad | Confusion kills conversion; the algorithm can't optimise for competing actions | One ad, one job, one CTA, always |

## 12. Generator-specific prompting guidance

Image prompt style should adapt to the generator being used. If the generator is not specified in the project config, use generator-agnostic language (the 7-element formula in `skills/creative-prompts-json/SKILL.md` works for all). When the generator IS known, apply these adaptations.

### Midjourney (v6.1 / v7)
- **Best for:** Cinematic aesthetics, fashion editorial, lifestyle, emotional storytelling, luxury visual register.
- **Prompt style:** Describe the *result* (what the image looks like), not the *process* (what to do). Comma-separated noun phrases work better than full sentences.
- **Key technique:** Avoid overly long prompts, verbose prompts with 10+ elements produce 54% muddled results (v7 data). Keep core prompt focused; use `--style raw` for product accuracy (removes Midjourney's aesthetic interpretation filter).
- **Add to any Midjourney prompt:** `--ar 4:5` for feed, `--ar 9:16` for stories, `--style raw` for commercial product fidelity.
- **What it handles badly:** Exact product label/text accuracy; precise composition instructions; readable typography.

### Flux.1 (Black Forest Labs: Pro / Dev)
- **Best for:** Photorealistic product hero shots, precise compositional control, text-in-image, editorial with technical accuracy.
- **Prompt style:** Natural language, instruction-following, write as a creative brief, not a comma list. Flux reads direct instructions literally ("product centered, upper third empty for text").
- **Key technique:** You can specify compositional layout explicitly. "Product lower-left frame, right two-thirds clean negative space for headline overlay" works as intended.
- **Film stocks:** Flux responds especially well to film stock names (Kodak Portra 400, Cinestill 800T) for color character.
- **Typography:** Flux.1 Pro supports text instructions with quotation notation: `text: "LOUTSA" in sans-serif, white, centered upper third`.
- **What it handles badly:** Highly stylized or painterly aesthetics; "mood" and emotion are less consistent than Midjourney.

### DALL-E 3 / GPT-4o image generation
- **Best for:** Rapid iteration, composition-following, product consistency across a batch session.
- **Prompt style:** Full natural language sentences. ChatGPT auto-rewrites vague prompts, prefix with "Do not modify or reinterpret this prompt:" when precision is required.
- **GPT-4o key advantage:** Upload a product reference image in the first turn; subsequent turns maintain product appearance across variations. Excellent for batch variants of the same brief.
- **What it handles badly:** Less photorealistic ceiling than Flux; images can look "produced" rather than photographed; UGC-style feel is harder to achieve.

### Generator-agnostic best practices (always apply)
1. **Physics-first, not adjectives:** "Soft diffuse window light from camera-left" beats "warm and cozy lighting." Describe what the light source is, where it comes from, and its quality.
2. **Camera + lens signals quality tier:** Include at minimum a focal length equivalent. Better: include a camera brand reference to signal the visual register.
3. **Maximum 2 strong style anchors.** More than 2 style references produce averaged, muddled results across all generators.
4. **One explicit exclusion.** State one thing NOT in the frame. Prevents the most predictable default compositions.
5. **Specify negative space.** Every ad prompt should include a negative-space instruction for text overlay: "upper third empty," "left column clean," "lower quarter gradient for text."
